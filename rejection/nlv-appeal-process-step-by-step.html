Spain NLV Appeal Process Step-by-Step: Filing a Recurso de Alzada

Once you've decided to appeal your NLV rejection, you're filing a formal recurso de alzada (administrative appeal) under Spanish law. This isn't a casual letter of complaint — it's a structured legal document with specific formatting, content, and submission requirements. This guide walks you through each step, from gathering evidence to submitting your appeal before the one-month deadline.

Understanding the Recurso de Alzada

A recurso de alzada is a formal appeal under Spanish administrative law (Ley 39/2015 del Procedimiento Administrativo Común). It challenges the consulate's original decision by providing new evidence or clarifying why the refusal decision was wrong.

Key points: The appeal goes to the same consulate that rejected you (they review their own decision). It must be submitted within 30 days of notification. The consulate has 2-3 months to respond. Your appeal must address the specific grounds stated in your refusal letter — you can't introduce unrelated arguments.

Step 1: Gather Your Evidence (Days 1-5 After Rejection)

Review Your Refusal Letter Carefully

Your refusal letter states the exact grounds for rejection. This is your roadmap. Read it multiple times and identify:

  • The specific requirement you failed to meet (not a general statement, but the exact issue)
  • What documentation or proof was missing
  • Whether consulate cited a misunderstanding or a documentation problem
  • Any specific questions consulate raised that you didn't answer

Identify What New Evidence You Can Provide

Your appeal needs new or clarifying evidence. This could include:

  • New documents: Correct health insurance, updated bank statements, new employment letter
  • Clarifying evidence: Explanation letters, certified copies of documents, professional translations
  • Context: Letters from banks, insurers, employers confirming why the original documentation was incomplete
  • Proof of error: If the refusal was based on misinterpretation, evidence that you actually meet the requirement

Critical point: Your new evidence must relate directly to the refusal grounds. You can't introduce a completely different angle or entirely new category of evidence.

Organize Evidence Into Categories

Create a folder structure:

  • Original submission: Copy of what you originally submitted
  • Refusal letter: The original rejection (reference it constantly in appeal)
  • New evidence: Organized by category (financial, insurance, documents, etc.)
  • Translations: If documents are in English, you'll need certified Spanish translations
  • Explanatory letters: Your written explanations of what went wrong and why new evidence fixes it

Step 2: Prepare Your Appeal Letter (Days 6-15)

Structure of the Appeal Letter

Your appeal letter must follow a standard Spanish administrative format:

Header Section

  • Date
  • Your full name and nationality
  • Your address (UK address if appealing from UK)
  • Consulate name and location
  • Subject line: "Recurso de Alzada contra Resolución de Denegación de Visado de No Lucrativo" (Administrative Appeal Against Non-Lucrative Visa Rejection)

Opening Paragraph (Expone/States)

State who you are and why you're submitting this appeal:

"I am [Name], [nationality], passport number [X], who was refused the Spanish Non-Lucrative Visa on [date] by consulate [location]. I am submitting this formal appeal (recurso de alzada) under Spanish Administrative Procedure Law (Ley 39/2015) within the 30-day statutory deadline."

Grounds Section (Hechos/Facts)

Restate the grounds of the refusal and explain why consulate's decision was wrong or incomplete:

  • Quote the specific reason given in your refusal letter
  • Explain whether it was a misunderstanding, missing documentation, or documentation error
  • State that you now have evidence addressing this issue

"The refusal letter states that my health insurance did not meet NLV requirements due to co-payment provisions. However, I have now obtained compliant insurance from [Provider] with zero co-payments, as verified by [Insurance Company] in writing. This resolves the stated deficiency."

Step 3: Spanish Translation Requirements

Your entire appeal must be submitted in Spanish. This is a legal requirement. If you are not fluent in Spanish administrative language, hire a professional translator certified in Spanish (jurado traductor).

What needs translation: Your appeal letter, all explanatory documents, any evidence originally in English. Translation certificates (apostilled) should accompany each document.

Step 4: Compile Your Appeal Package (Days 16-25)

Your complete dossier should include:

  • Your original appeal letter (in Spanish, signed and dated)
  • Copy of your original refusal letter
  • Copy of your original NLV application
  • New evidence, organized by category
  • Professional translations of all documents in English
  • Explanatory letters addressing each ground of refusal
  • Any professional certifications or correspondence supporting your evidence

Create a table of contents listing every document and its page numbers. Number every page. This shows professionalism and makes the consulate's job easier.

Step 5: Submit Your Appeal (Days 26-30)

Submission method matters. Options, in order of preference:

  1. In person at the consulate — Get a dated receipt showing your name, application reference, and submission date. This is the safest proof.
  2. Registered mail with tracking — Use a tracked service (Royal Mail Special Delivery, equivalent in your country). Keep your receipt showing the delivery date and tracking number.
  3. Consulate online system — Some consulates accept submissions through their website. Document the submission date and confirmation email.
  4. Email is NOT acceptable — Many consulates explicitly state email submissions do not constitute a valid appeal submission. Do not rely on email alone.

Critical timing: Don't wait until day 30. If you're submitting by post, send your appeal on day 25 at the latest to ensure it arrives within the deadline. If submitting in person, schedule your appointment well before day 30.

Post-Submission Timeline

  • Days 1-3: Consulate receives and logs your appeal
  • Days 4-14: Initial review of your submission for completeness
  • Days 15-50: In-depth review of the original decision and your new evidence
  • Days 51-90: Final decision preparation
  • Day 90 (latest): Decision issued and mailed to you

Real timelines vary by consulate. Some fast consulates (Madrid, Barcelona) may decide within 4-6 weeks. High-volume consulates (London, New York) may take 2-3 months.

Common Mistakes in Appeal Submissions

  1. Missing the deadline. The 30-day deadline is absolute. Missing it is catastrophic — your right to appeal is gone permanently. Mark your calendar. Set reminders.
  2. Submitting an appeal that doesn't address the refusal grounds. If your refusal was about health insurance and you submit a document about income, the appeal will fail. Every argument in your appeal must directly address what consulate said was wrong.
  3. Providing no new evidence. An appeal without new evidence is just complaining. You must provide evidence you didn't provide before — corrected documents, new certifications, explanatory letters, or proof of error.
  4. Poor translation quality. A translation with errors or legal-language mistakes will undermine your credibility. Hire a professional jurado traductor. It's worth €50–100 per page.
  5. Submitting original documents instead of certified copies. Never send original passports, certificates, or important documents. The consulate may not return them. Always send certified copies (apostilled if required).
  6. Forgetting to include the original refusal letter. Your appeal references the refusal grounds. Include a copy of the refusal letter so the consulate can refer back to it as they read your appeal. Makes their job easier.
  7. Submitting email when the consulate requires registered mail or in-person. Check your consulate's website for their preferred submission method. Email is almost never acceptable for legal appeals.
  8. Not keeping proof of submission. You need dated evidence that your appeal was submitted on time. In-person: get a receipt. Mail: keep tracking number and photo of the tracking label. Online system: screenshot the confirmation.

What Happens Next?

After you submit, you wait. The consulate will:

  1. Log your appeal and assign it a reference number
  2. Re-examine the original refusal decision
  3. Review your new evidence
  4. Determine whether the original decision should be upheld, reversed, or whether more information is needed
  5. Issue a written decision

You'll receive a decision letter by post. It will either approve your appeal (visa issued), uphold the refusal, or request additional information (with a new deadline).

If Your Appeal Is Denied

If the consulate upholds the refusal, your options are:

  • Reapply from scratch. Address the root cause of rejection. It's better than appealing again on the same grounds.
  • Appeal the appeal decision. If you have entirely new grounds or evidence, you can appeal the second refusal. But consulates are unlikely to reverse themselves twice on the same issue.
  • Seek judicial review. In rare cases, applicants have pursued judicial review through Spanish courts. This is expensive, slow, and rarely successful.
